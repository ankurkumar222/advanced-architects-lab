step 01

Functional & non-functional requirements

Imagine you're building your own Kafka for a banking/wealth platform. Functional requirements say what it must do. Non-functional requirements say how well it must do it. Everything downstream will be judged against these.

✎ build it yourself first

Write your own FR list (5–7 bullets) and NFR targets with real numbers — throughput, latency, your durability/availability trade-off — for this log system inside your domain. Then write one line on what you're explicitly scoping OUT, and why leaving it out is itself a design decision.

1. Producers should be able to send messages

  Order Created --> Payment Received --> Order Completed

  Order Created --> Payment Received --> Order Completed

NEVER like this:

Simple meaning: whatever order messages are written in, they must be read in that same order.

Simple meaning: every consumer reads at its own speed.

        *** crash ***
Restart --> "where was I?" --> offset says 5 --> resume from [6]

NOT from [1]. NOT from some random spot.
Simple meaning: after a crash, continue where you stopped.
5. New consumers should be able to read old messages 
Jan --- Feb --- Mar --- ... --- Jul
                                  |
                          New Audit Service
                          created in July
                                  |
                     "give me everything from Jan"
                                  |
                    Kafka still has it (within retention)
Simple meaning: new applications can replay old data.
6. Every client should know where data is stored 
Producer: "Where is Orders Partition 2?"
                    |
                    v
Kafka: "Broker 3"
                    |
                    v
Producer sends DIRECTLY to Broker 3 (no guessing)
Simple meaning: clients can discover which broker owns which partition.
7. Don't say "success" until data is safely stored 
Producer sends "Order Created"
        |
Broker 1 receives it --> crashes immediately

IF Kafka already said "SUCCESS"  --> data is LOST, producer thinks it's safe ✗

INSTEAD, Kafka waits:
  Broker 1 ✓ --> Broker 2 ✓ --> Broker 3 ✓ --> NOW reply "SUCCESS"
Simple meaning: tell the producer "success" only after the data is safely replicated.
Why this matters: Kafka doesn't have one durability setting for the whole cluster — it's per-topic. Writing out each FR like this, one small scene at a time, is what makes you notice that "the system" doesn't have one behavior; your topics do. That realization drives almost everything downstream.

1. Throughput 
Normal day:         500 msgs/sec     [====------------------]
Busy market hours: 3,500 msgs/sec   [==============--------]
Future growth:      10,000 msgs/sec  [======================]
Simple meaning: handle lots of messages without slowing down.
2. Latency 
9:00:00.000  producer sends
9:00:00.030  Kafka stores it        (30 ms)

Simple meaning: messages should move through Kafka very quickly.
3. Critical topics should never lose data 
Topic: money.transfer

Replicas available?  NO
        |
        v
REJECT the write, return an error   (not: silently accept and risk loss)
Simple meaning: for important data, it's better to reject writes than lose data.
4. Less important topics can prioritize availability 
Topic: ui.click.events

One message lost? --> shrug, keep going, app stays online
Simple meaning: for non-critical data, staying online matters more than perfect durability.
Why different settings per topic? 
Topic                    Importance     Preferred behavior
--------------------------------------------------------------------
payment.transactions     Very High      never lose data, even reject writes
orders                   High           strong durability
notifications            Medium         small delays/rare loss OK
ui.click.events          Low            keep accepting, occasional loss OK
Why this matters: not one rule for the whole cluster — each topic gets its own dial. That's the concrete mechanism behind "durability > availability for money, availability > durability for clicks" — it's a per-topic config (acks, min.insync.replicas), not a cluster-wide switch.
R3What should you explicitly scope OUT — and why does that matter as much as what's in scope?reveal
Scope out: stream processing / joins / windowed aggregation (that's a layer built on this system, e.g. Kafka Streams/Flink — not the log itself), schema enforcement (that's a registry sitting beside the broker, not inside it), and exactly-once cross-topic transactions (a significant added protocol on top of the base replication model).
Why this matters: every one of those is a legitimate, common ask — and every one of them, if you try to build it into segment 1, will silently bend your component boundaries in segment 3 before you've even validated the core log works. Scoping out isn't laziness — it's protecting the shape of the core design from feature creep disguised as a requirement.
step 02

Scope & the core design decision

One or two decisions here will shape every segment after this. Get them wrong and you'll be fighting your own architecture by segment 6.

✎ build it yourself first

Commit, in writing, to: (1) your ordering guarantee scope — global or per-partition-key? (2) your delivery semantics — at-least-once, at-most-once, or exactly-once, and where the cost of that choice lands. Justify both against your own segment-1 numbers before revealing.

R3Reference decision — ordering scope.reveal
Per-partition-key ordering only. No global order across a whole topic. 
GLOBAL ORDER (one writer for the whole topic):
  Topic: [msg1][msg2][msg3][msg4][msg5]   <- single line, single writer
  Throughput capped at ONE partition's worth of I/O

PER-PARTITION-KEY ORDER (what we pick):
  Partition 0 (key=folio_A): [A1][A2][A3]     <- always in order for folio_A
  Partition 1 (key=folio_B): [B1][B2][B3]     <- always in order for folio_B
  Partition 2 (key=folio_C): [C1][C2]         <- always in order for folio_C

  Many partitions write in PARALLEL -> throughput scales
  But there's no single line across all of them -> no global order
Why this matters: global order needs one writer for the whole topic — that single-lane bottleneck directly breaks segment 1's throughput target. Routing every event for a key to the same partition gives you order exactly where it's needed (per folio, per account) without paying for order where it isn't (across unrelated customers).
R4Reference decision — delivery semantics.reveal
At-least-once everywhere, by default. Exactly-once only opt-in, for the narrow slice that truly can't tolerate a duplicate (settlement writes). 
AT-LEAST-ONCE (default, cheap):
  message sent -> maybe delivered twice on retry -> consumer must dedupe
  cost: consumer does idempotent writes            speed: fast

EXACTLY-ONCE (opt-in, expensive):
  message sent -> transactional producer + read-committed consumer
  cost: extra coordination on EVERY write           speed: slower

  Applying exactly-once to EVERY topic:
    ui.click.events   <- pays exactly-once tax it never needed  ✗
    settlement.writes <- actually needs it                       ✓
Why this matters: making exactly-once the platform default means every topic — including low-stakes ones like UI clicks — pays extra latency for a guarantee most of them never asked for. Opt-in per topic keeps the platform's baseline fast and lets the rare, genuinely expensive requirement pay for itself.

R6Why are Append Path and Replication Manager separate? (the most important decision in this whole segment)reveal
If they were combined: 
Producer
   |
   v
Write locally (1 ms)
   |
   v
Wait for Follower A ack (30 ms over network)
   |
   v
Wait for Follower B ack (30 ms over network)
   |
   v
Return Success

TOTAL: 31+ ms, EVERY single write, blocked on network round-trips
Separated instead: 
STEP 1 (fast, hot path):
Producer --> Append Path --> write to local disk --> Done   (~1 ms)

STEP 2 (in parallel, independent):
Leader --> copy to Follower A
Leader --> copy to Follower B
(this happens WHILE the next producer write is already being appended)
Append PathReplication Manager
Writes to local diskTalks to other brokers over network
Microseconds to a few msTens of ms, depends on network
Performance-critical ("hot path")Reliability-focused
Goal: write as fast as possibleGoal: keep replicas in sync
Easy analogy — Amazon warehouse: 
1. Truck arrives with packages.
2. Worker scans and shelves each package immediately.        <- Append Path
3. Another team later copies inventory to backup warehouses.  <- Replication Manager
4. Customers order, staff pick items from shelves.             <- Fetch Path
5. Manager tracks which warehouse stores which products.       <- Controller / Metadata
6. Supervisor assigns picking work to different employees.     <- Consumer Group Coordinator
If the same worker had to scan the package, personally drive it to every backup warehouse, and only then accept the next package — the whole warehouse grinds to a halt.
Why this matters: separating them is what lets Kafka get both high throughput and high durability, instead of picking one. This is also exactly why segment 1's sub-50ms latency target and 3.5k msgs/sec throughput target are both achievable at once — they'd directly conflict if append and replication were the same blocking call.

Purpose
Write a message into a topic. Without this, no application can write data.
Caller
Any producer service — order-service, nsdl-adapter, nav-ingest-job.

Customer 101 orders, all hashed the same way:

  Order 1 --> hash(101) --> Partition 4
  Order 2 --> hash(101) --> Partition 4
  Order 3 --> hash(101) --> Partition 4

Partition 4 log:  [Order 1][Order 2][Order 3]   <- stays in order
Why idempotency matters: 
Producer sends "Order Created" -----> (network blip, no response)
Producer thinks: "maybe it never landed" -----> retries

WITHOUT idempotency:
  Partition:  [Order Created][Order Created]     <- duplicate!

WITH idempotency (producer_id=100, sequence=55):
  Kafka checks memory:  (100,55) already stored?
     yes -> ignore retry
  Partition:  [Order Created]                     <- safe
GET/fetch
Purpose
Consumers need a way to read. Kafka never pushes data — the consumer always decides where to start.
Caller
Consumer client library — settlement-svc, reporting-pipeline.
settlement-svc: "Give me messages starting from offset 200"
Kafka returns: 200, 201, 202, 203
Offset is just a bookmark on a book: 
Partition (like pages in a book):
[0][1][2][3][4][5][6][7]
          ^
     consumer already read up to page 3

Next fetch --> "start from 4" --> Kafka returns [4][5][6][7]
Why it's idempotent: 
Fetch(offset=100) today     --> returns [100][101][102]
Fetch(offset=100) next week --> returns [100][101][102]   <- identical, nothing changed
RPCOffsetCommit()
Purpose
One of the most important APIs — persists "this group has processed up to here."
Caller
Consumer client library, on its own commit cadence — not per record.
Consumer processed offsets 0,1,2,3
Consumer tells Kafka: "I'm done till 3"
The bookmark / crash-resume story: 
BEFORE crash:
  processed: [0][1][2][3]  --> OffsetCommit(offset=3) --> stored durably

  *** server crashes ***

AFTER restart:
  "Where was I?"  --> read stored offset --> "3"
  "Resume from 4"  --> Fetch(offset=4)
Why we don't commit every message: 
1,000,000 messages/sec

Commit EVERY message:
  msg -> commit -> msg -> commit -> msg -> commit ...
  = 1,000,000 storage writes/sec   <- too slow

Commit every 1,000 messages (or every 5 sec):
  msg msg msg ... msg(1000) -> ONE commit
  = 1,000 storage writes/sec        <- 1000x cheaper
RPCJoinGroup()
Purpose
The one that confuses people first — decides who reads which partition.
Caller
Consumer client library, on startup or after a heartbeat timeout.
Topic: 8 partitions
Consumers: A, B, C — group "Payment"
Partition assignment: 
Topic "orders" -- 8 partitions: [0][1][2][3][4][5][6][7]

Consumer A --JoinGroup--> Kafka --assigns--> [1][4][7]
Consumer B --JoinGroup--> Kafka --assigns--> [2][5]
Consumer C --JoinGroup--> Kafka --assigns--> [0][3][6]

                (no overlap, every partition owned once)
Rebalance when B dies: 
BEFORE:                       AFTER (B dies):
A -> [1][4][7]                A -> [1][2][4][5][7]
B -> [2][5]        --dies-->  B -> (gone)
C -> [0][3][6]                C -> [0][3][6]

               ^ partition 2, 5 reassigned to A
Why heartbeats exist separately: 
WITHOUT heartbeat (using JoinGroup to say "I'm alive"):
  Join -> rebalance -> Join -> rebalance -> Join -> rebalance ...
  (expensive, disruptive)

WITH heartbeat:
  Heartbeat -> "still here" -> Heartbeat -> "still here" ...
  (cheap, no rebalance triggered)
  JoinGroup only fires on real join/leave/crash
ADMINCreateTopic()
Purpose
Admin-only. Application services never call this directly.
Caller
A human operator or a CI/CD provisioning pipeline.
Operator: "Create topic 'orders', 8 partitions, replication 3"
Kafka: creates orders → Partition 0...7
Why conflicting re-creation is rejected, not merged: 
Day 1:  orders created with 4 partitions
        customer101 -> hash(101) % 4 -> Partition 1
        Partition 1 log: [order-A][order-B]

Day 30: someone tries "orders, 12 partitions"
        customer101 -> hash(101) % 12 -> Partition 9   <- different!

        Partition 1: [order-A][order-B]   <- old orders, stranded
        Partition 9: [order-C]            <- new orders, different partition

        Same customer's history now SPLIT across two partitions.
        Kafka rejects the second CreateTopic call to prevent this.
ADMINDescribeCluster()
Purpose
Producer can't guess which broker holds a partition — it asks.
Caller
Producer and consumer clients, plus operator tooling.
Producer: "Where is Partition 0? Who's the leader?"
The map: 
DescribeCluster() ------> Kafka replies:

   orders / Partition 0  --> Broker 2
   orders / Partition 1  --> Broker 5
   orders / Partition 2  --> Broker 1

Producer caches this map:
   Produce(partition 1) --> goes DIRECTLY to Broker 5
   (no need to ask again next time)
Why it's necessary at scale: 
Cluster with 100 brokers, no map:
  Producer: "???" -----> which of 100 brokers do I even talk to?

Cluster with DescribeCluster():
  Producer: ask once -----> cache map -----> route directly, every time after
Bonus — blast radius, not ownership: 
Team owns 10 topics, including:
  payment.transactions
  ui.click.events

Scoped by OWNERSHIP:
  any team credential --> can write to BOTH topics
  (a bug in analytics-service could corrupt payment data)

Scoped by BLAST RADIUS:
  payment-service credential   --> can write ONLY to payment.transactions
  analytics-service credential --> can write ONLY to ui.click.events
  (a leaked credential or bug damages only what it was meant to touch)
"Who owns this API" tells you who to page. "What's the blast radius if this call is misused" tells you how to scope the token — and that's the question that actually protects the system.

R8What's durable vs. ephemeral — and why mixing them up is a mistake?reveal
DURABLE (on disk, survives a restart, the real source of truth):
  - log segments
  - offset indexes
  - committed consumer offsets

EPHEMERAL (in-memory, just a fast cache, rebuilt on restart):
  - "which broker is leader for this partition, right now"
  - consumer group's current in-memory assignment
  - ISR membership snapshot
Why conflating them is a mistake: if a broker restart accidentally treats its in-memory ISR snapshot as truth instead of re-checking with the controller, it can either wrongly distrust a replica that was actually fine (wasted re-catch-up) or wrongly trust a stale one (real data-loss risk). The controller is the only durable source of "who's really in sync" — everything else is a disposable cache of that fact, not truth itself.
R9Gut check — is this actually a "big data" problem?reveal
~570 GB/day replicated x 7-day retention
  = ~4 TB steady-state per replicated topic set

That's comfortably single-digit-node storage.
NOT a "shard across 200 machines" problem.
Why this matters: the actual hard part here isn't volume — it's throughput + ordering + durability under concurrent access. That's a systems problem, not a big-data problem. Don't let the word "Kafka" trick you into over-building for scale you don't actually have yet.
step 06

Core algorithm: the append + ISR replication protocol

This is the heart of Kafka. Everything about durability depends on one question: when should Kafka tell the producer "your message is safely stored"?

Producer receives: SUCCESS   --> safe to continue
R11What is ISR (In-Sync Replicas), and what happens when a follower falls behind?reveal
ISR = the followers fully caught up with the leader. 
Leader:     offset 100
Follower A: offset 100
Follower B: offset 100

ISR = { Leader, Follower A, Follower B }    <- everyone healthy
If one follower falls behind: 
Leader:     offset 105
Follower A: offset 105
Follower B: offset 98      <- too far behind

ISR = { Leader, Follower A }     <- B removed, not trusted yet
      (B keeps copying in the background, just not counted for acks)
How it comes back: 
Follower B: 98 -> 99 -> 100 -> 101 -> ... -> 105   <- catches up

Kafka: "Welcome back!"
ISR = { Leader, Follower A, Follower B }
R12The three ACK modes — what's the tradeoff of each?reveal
acks = 0  (fastest, riskiest)
  Producer --> send --> continue immediately, doesn't wait at all

acks = 1  (safer)
  Producer --> Leader Disk --> ACK
  (followers may not have copied yet)

acks = all  (slowest, safest)
  Producer --> Leader --> Follower A --> Follower B --> ACK
  (leader waits for every replica in the ISR)
Why this matters: this one setting is the concrete dial behind segment 1's "durability > availability for money, availability > durability for clicks" — acks=all for settlement topics, acks=0 or 1 for low-stakes ones like UI clicks.
R13What if the followers disappear entirely — leader alone, min.insync.replicas=2, acks=all?reveal
Leader:     ✓ up
Follower A: ❌ down
Follower B: ❌ down

Only the Leader remains. Setting: min.insync.replicas=2, acks=all

Kafka: ❌ "Not Enough Replicas — Write Rejected"
Why reject instead of just accepting it anyway? 
IF Kafka accepted the write anyway:
  Leader saves message --> Leader crashes immediately after
  --> no copy exists anywhere --> money transaction lost forever

INSTEAD, Kafka is honest:
  "Sorry, I cannot safely store your message. Please retry later."
Why this matters: refusing the write is a worse experience in the moment, but a strictly better guarantee for the business — this is exactly what protects a settlement pipeline from silently lying about durability.
R14Bonus, for the technically curious: what does this look like as actual pseudocode?reveal
// Leader-side state per partition
struct PartitionState {
  log: AppendOnlyLog          // local durable segment writer
  isr: Set<ReplicaId>         // currently in-sync replicas, includes self
  high_watermark: Offset      // highest offset acked by ALL of isr
  lock: SingleWriterLock       // one append at a time, this partition only
}

// 1. Append (hot path — must stay fast)
function append(record, acks_mode):
  lock.acquire()
  offset = log.write(record)       // sequential disk write, local only
  lock.release()
  if acks_mode == "all":
    return wait_until(offset <= high_watermark)   // blocks until ISR catches up
  return ack_immediately()

// 2. Follower replication loop (async, off the hot path)
function follower_replicate_loop(follower):
  while true:
    records = leader.log.read_from(follower.last_replicated_offset + 1)
    follower.log.write(records)
    follower.last_replicated_offset += len(records)

// 3. ISR membership check (leader-side, periodic — NOT inside the append lock)
function recompute_high_watermark_and_isr():
  for replica in isr:
    if replica.lag_ms() > threshold_ms: isr.remove(replica)
  high_watermark = min(replica.last_replicated_offset for replica in isr)
Why append and ISR-recompute are separate functions: the append path needs to stay a fast local write held under lock for microseconds. If ISR bookkeeping (network checks, lag calculation) happened inside that same lock, every append would serialize behind the slowest follower's heartbeat — exactly the coupling segment 3 said to avoid.

R18Why doesn't a single, centralized coordinator become a bottleneck?reveal
The Coordinator does NOT process messages.
It only acts when:
  - a consumer joins
  - a consumer leaves
  - a consumer crashes

These events are RARE.

Actual message flow:
  Producer --> Broker --> Consumer     (Coordinator is NOT in this path)
Why this matters: the coordinator never moves data — it only decides who reads what. That's exactly why it can stay centralized without becoming a performance bottleneck; centralizing a rare, infrequent decision is cheap, while centralizing the hot data path would not be.
R19Bonus, for the technically curious: the coordinator loop as pseudocode.reveal
// Runs on one broker elected as coordinator for this group_id
function coordinator_loop(group):
  loop every heartbeat_check_interval:
    for member in group.members:
      if now() - member.last_heartbeat > member.session_timeout_ms:
        group.members.remove(member)
        trigger_rebalance(group)          // member presumed dead

  on receive JoinGroup(member_id):
    group.members.add(member_id)
    trigger_rebalance(group)              // new member -> reassign

  on receive Heartbeat(member_id):
    group.members[member_id].last_heartbeat = now()

function trigger_rebalance(group):
  group.state = REBALANCING
  partitions = all_partitions(group.subscribed_topics)
  assignment = partition_assignor(partitions, group.members)  // e.g. sticky/round-robin
  broadcast_assignment(assignment)
  group.state = STABLE
Centralized per group, not distributed consensus: one broker acts as coordinator for a given group_id, chosen deterministically. A fully distributed protocol among consumers (gossip/voting) trades this for network-partition-tolerant consensus — solving a much harder problem for a decision that's rare and just needs to converge fast, not sustain high QPS. The real risk isn't centralization, it's a rebalance storm — too many join/leave events too close together — which is a tuning problem (session timeout, static membership), not an architecture problem.

R20Reference: three state machines, one at a time.reveal
A partition, from a broker's point of view: 
[Offline] --controller assigns--> [Becoming-Leader] --logs recovered--> [Leader]
[Offline] --controller assigns--> [Follower] --catches up--> [In-Sync Follower]
[Leader]/[Follower] --broker unreachable--> [Offline] --> controller re-elects
A replica, from the leader's point of view (this is the ISR dance from segment 6): 
[Catching-Up] --lag below threshold--> [In-Sync, ISR member]
[In-Sync] --lag exceeds threshold--> [Catching-Up] --removed from ISR--
[Catching-Up] --never catches up (timeout)--> [Considered Dead]
A consumer group (this is the rebalance loop from segment 7): 
[Stable] --member join/leave/crash--> [Rebalancing]
[Rebalancing] --new assignment broadcast + acked--> [Stable]
[Rebalancing] --rebalance timeout exceeded--> [Rebalancing]  (retry, with backoff)
R21Where's the "stuck forever" risk, and how do you close it?reveal
RISK 1: [Rebalancing] loops forever
  if members keep joining/leaving faster than assignment can stabilize
  (a "rebalance storm")

RISK 2: [Becoming-Leader] stalls forever
  if log recovery hangs on a corrupted segment
The fix, same shape both times — a timeout that forces an exit no matter what: 
[Becoming-Leader] --timeout--> "mark partition unavailable, alert operator"
  (instead of hanging forever waiting for recovery to "succeed")

[Rebalancing] --backoff + max-attempts ceiling--> kick the flapping member out
  (instead of blocking the whole group's progress indefinitely)
Why this matters: a state machine that only moves forward on "success" events has no way out of a state where success never comes. Every forced exit above is only safe because the operation being retried afterward — fetch, produce with a sequence number, offset commit — is idempotent. A forced retry after a timeout never double-applies an effect; that's the retry/idempotency plug-in point.
step 09

Scaling this component itself

Not the systems this log serves — this log system. How do more brokers and a bigger controller stay coordinated without becoming the bottleneck.

✎ build it yourself first

Decide: as broker count grows, what keeps controller/metadata decisions from becoming a coordination bottleneck? Then write one concrete metric, tied back to your segment-1 NFRs, that would tell you "it's time to scale out."

R22Reference: what stops the controller from becoming the bottleneck as brokers grow?reveal
Move 1 — a small quorum, not one node: 
ONE controller node:
  fast, but a single point of failure

Quorum of 3-5 nodes (Raft-style — what KRaft replaced ZooKeeper with):
  fault-tolerant, without needing "everyone votes on everything"
Move 2 — treat metadata itself as a log, don't push it synchronously: 
NAIVE (synchronous push, doesn't scale):
  every metadata change --> controller waits for ALL brokers to confirm
  420 partitions --> 420x the chatter on every leadership change   ✗

BETTER (metadata as its own log, brokers tail it):
  controller writes change --> metadata log
  each broker --> reads/caches, re-fetches only on a version bump
  metadata update becomes an O(1) "check my cached version" read   ✓
Why this matters: this is the exact same trick the data plane uses — append-only log + independent readers — just applied to the control plane instead of order data. The naive synchronous design turns partition count into a coordination-cost multiplier; the log-based design doesn't.
R23What's the one metric that tells you "it's time to scale out"?reveal
Watch for:
  under-replicated partition count > 0, sustained for 2+ minutes
  OR
  controller request queue depth trending up across 3+ monitoring windows

NOT:
  raw CPU / disk usage alone
  (a cluster can look "healthy" on CPU right up until replication falls behind)
Why this matters: under-replicated partitions is causally close to your actual durability guarantee breaking — it gives you lead time to act, instead of a lagging confirmation that something already went wrong after your segment-1 latency target has already been breached.
