Stage 0

Don't say "reconciliation engine"

Architect A

I don't want a reconciliation engine. Convince me you even need one — why can't finance just eyeball the numbers at month end?

✎ think first

A single payment moves through a PSP, a bank, and your internal ledger. Each system records its own version of "what happened." How would you even notice if they disagree?

Reveal the dialoguereveal
B

Option 1: trust the ledger. If the ledger says money moved, assume it moved.

Bank        --> "payment settled, UTR=8827..."
PSP         --> "webhook fired, status=SUCCESS"
Our Ledger  --> "order marked PAID"

Three independent stories. Nobody is comparing them.
A

What actually breaks if you just trust the ledger?

B

The PSP webhook fires, our system marks the order PAID, but the bank settlement silently fails or gets reversed hours later. The ledger now says something that isn't true, and nothing forces it to notice.

A

Fine, so write a nightly SQL join between two tables and call it done. Why isn't that reconciliation?

B

Because the two tables don't even have matching granularity. A bank settlement file has one row per batch of 500 transactions; our ledger has one row per individual order. You can't join those on a common key — there isn't one yet.

BANK FILE (per batch):            LEDGER (per order):
  batch_id=B12, amount=₹4.8L         order_id=O991, amount=₹12,000
                                      order_id=O992, amount=₹8,500
                                      ... (500 more rows inside B12)
Where this leaves us: reconciliation isn't a nice-to-have check — it's the only mechanism that forces three independently-written stories about the same money to agree, or tells you exactly where they don't. Everything that follows is really about how to make that comparison possible at all.
Stage 1

What are we optimizing?

Architect A

Pick one, only one: speed, completeness, or correctness. You can't maximize all three.

✎ think first

Compare the cost of a recon system that's slow-but-right against one that's fast-but-occasionally-wrong. Which failure mode is more dangerous for money?

Reveal the dialoguereveal
B

Correctness. Not speed.

A

Why not speed? Finance wants same-day answers.

B

Because a fast, wrong match is worse than a slow, right one. If the system confidently marks two unrelated transactions as reconciled, that discrepancy is now invisible — nobody will ever look at it again. A slow match just means the answer arrives late; it never lies.

A

Give me a concrete case where being fast-but-wrong actually costs money.

B

Mutual fund units are allotted based on NAV cut-off once a payment is confirmed. If a premature or incorrect match marks a failed payment as settled, units get allotted against money that was never actually received — and units, unlike a database row, can't just be silently reversed.

Where this leaves us: correctness-first isn't a soft preference — it's the constraint that shapes the entire matching design that follows. Every later stage — matching thresholds, break states, audit trails — exists to protect this one priority, never to make the system faster at the expense of it.
Stage 2

What is a "transaction record," really?

Architect A

You'll be comparing a bank MT940 file, a PSP webhook JSON, and an internal Kafka event. Same shape?

✎ think first

If three sources describe the same event in three different formats, where does the comparison logic actually live — inside each pairwise comparison, or somewhere earlier?

Reveal the dialoguereveal
B

Completely different shapes.

BANK MT940:        :61:250714C12000NTRFUTR8827...
PSP WEBHOOK:        {"txn_ref":"pay_9F2","status":"success","amt":1200000}
INTERNAL EVENT:     {order_id: O991, ledger_state: PAID, ts: ...}
A

So store all three raw and write comparison logic that understands each one?

B

No — that means every matching rule needs three branches, and every new source doubles the branching again. Normalize first: parse each source into one canonical schema — reference id, counterparty reference, amount, currency, timestamp, source system, direction — before any comparison touches it.

A

What if the bank never returns a reference id you generated — only one it generated?

B

Then the canonical schema has to carry both fields separately: our_ref and counterparty_ref. Force-fitting them into one field is exactly the kind of shortcut that breaks matching two stages from now.

Where this leaves us: the normalization layer built here is what makes matching (stage 3) tractable at all — without it, every comparison is a one-off special case instead of a single, reusable rule.
Stage 3

The matching key

Architect A

Just match on transaction ID. Why doesn't that work on day one?

✎ think first

Does the bank ever see the order ID your system generated? If not, what does it actually key its records on?

Reveal the dialoguereveal
B

The bank never sees our internal order ID. It only knows the UTR or RRN it generated itself. There's no shared key by default.

A

So generate one and force it through?

B

Right — a correlation ID has to be propagated end-to-end at payment initiation, embedded in the narration or remarks field of the payment instruction. That's the only field that survives the trip through the bank's systems and comes back to us.

INITIATION:
  Our order O991 --> payment instruction --> narration: "RECON:O991"

BANK SETTLEMENT FILE (T+1):
  UTR=8827... narration="RECON:O991" amount=12,000

MATCH: narration field carries our key back to us.
A

What happens when the bank truncates that narration field to 20 characters?

B

Then it degrades, not breaks — fall back to a composite fuzzy match on amount, account number, and a time window. Weaker, but not blind.

Where this leaves us: matching quality is decided upstream, at payment initiation — not at matching time. If the correlation key isn't designed into the payment flow itself, no cleverness at the recon layer can fully recover it later.
Stage 4

The unavoidable trade-off: precision vs. recall

Architect A

Fuzzy matching will sometimes match the wrong pair and sometimes miss a real pair. Which failure do you tolerate?

✎ think first

Compare the actual downstream consequence of a false match versus a missed match. Which one can hide forever, and which one can't?

Reveal the dialoguereveal
B

Tolerate missed matches. Never tolerate a false match.

A

Why the asymmetry? Explain the actual cost of each, don't just assert it.

B

A false match takes two unrelated transactions, declares them reconciled, and closes the case. A real discrepancy — money short, money duplicated — now looks resolved and nobody ever looks at it again. A missed match just sits in a review queue, ugly but visible.

FALSE MATCH:           MISSED MATCH:
  [A] --matched--> [B]    [A]   ...unmatched...   [B]
  case CLOSED              case OPEN, flagged for review
  discrepancy: invisible   discrepancy: visible, recoverable
A

So tighten the fuzzy-match threshold to near-exact?

B

Yes — exact match preferred wherever the correlation key survives; fuzzy match only as a last resort, and even then it routes to mandatory manual confirmation rather than auto-closing.

Where this leaves us: precision-over-recall becomes the design principle behind everything downstream — the exception queue in stage 9, the immutable audit trail in stage 12, the split-match guardrails in stage 13. Every one of those exists because silence is the one outcome this system is never allowed to produce.
Stage 5

It's never just two sources

Architect A

A LAMF disbursement needs the bank's transfer, NSDL's lien marking on the mutual fund units, and our internal loan ledger to all agree. You've only designed for two-way matching. What now?

✎ think first

If two out of three sources agree and one doesn't, is that a "match" or a "break"? What does pairwise matching get wrong here?

Reveal the dialoguereveal
B

Pairwise 2-way matching hides which source is actually the problem. If bank-vs-ledger passes but NSDL-vs-ledger fails, running two separate pairwise jobs gives you two independent, disconnected answers instead of one clear picture.

PAIRWISE (blind to the third source):
  Bank <-> Ledger:   MATCH
  NSDL <-> Ledger:   BREAK      <- but WHY? bank says fine!

N-WAY (one status per correlation key):
  key=LOAN-4471: Bank=OK, Ledger=OK, NSDL=MISSING
  --> classify as "2-of-3 matched, NSDL pending" -- specific, actionable
A

So every correlation key needs a combinatorial status across all N sources, not a single matched/unmatched flag?

B

Exactly — matched-all, matched-partial (naming which source is missing), or unmatched-all. That third case, "2-of-3," is the one a binary system can't even represent.

A

NSDL settlement data lands T+1, PSP webhooks land in real time. Doesn't that create false "NSDL missing" alerts constantly?

B

It will — unless we're careful about when we're allowed to even ask the question. That's the next problem.

Where this leaves us: n-way reconciliation isn't matching two tables twice — it's a per-correlation-key state across every source, arriving on its own schedule. That timing problem is now unavoidable, and it's exactly what stage 7 has to solve.
Stage 6

Matched or unmatched isn't enough

Architect A

The moment a transaction enters the recon system, is it "matched" or "unmatched" — a binary?

✎ think first

What's the difference between a transaction that's still waiting for its counterpart to arrive, and one that's been waiting too long? Does the system need to know that difference?

Reveal the dialoguereveal
B

No — binary hides too much. It needs explicit states: PENDING (waiting for a counterpart), MATCHED, BREAK (counterpart arrived but disagrees), UNRESOLVED (past its expected window, still missing), UNDER_REVIEW (a human is on it), CLOSED.

PENDING --(counterpart arrives, agrees)--> MATCHED --> CLOSED
   |
   +--(counterpart arrives, disagrees)--> BREAK --> UNDER_REVIEW --> CLOSED
   |
   +--(window expires, nothing arrives)--> UNRESOLVED --> UNDER_REVIEW --> CLOSED
A

Why can't PENDING just retry forever until it eventually matches?

B

Because "still pending, forever" is indistinguishable from "genuinely lost money that nobody will ever chase." Every state needs an SLA-driven timeout that forces a transition and, past a threshold, alerts a human — silence can't be a valid resting state.

Where this leaves us: this state machine becomes the backbone every later mechanism hangs off — the exception queue in stage 9 is really just "who owns the UNDER_REVIEW transition," and the audit trail in stage 12 is "what happens the instant a record reaches CLOSED."
Stage 7

You can't join a table that doesn't exist yet

Architect A

NSDL settlement files land T+1. PSP webhooks land in under a second. A ledger entry is created today — the bank file won't exist until tomorrow. What do you do with it right now?

✎ think first

If "not found yet" and "actually missing" look identical at the moment you check, how do you tell them apart without waiting forever?

Reveal the dialoguereveal
B

Don't match at read time — window it. Every PENDING record gets an expected_arrival derived from its source's known settlement SLA. The matching job only evaluates "is this actually missing" after that window closes, never before.

TIMELINE for one correlation key:
  T+0   ledger entry created            --> state: PENDING
  T+0   PSP webhook arrives, matches    --> partial match recorded
  T+1   expected NSDL window opens
  T+1 (+grace)  NSDL file still absent  --> NOW eligible to flip UNRESOLVED
  T+0..T+1(+grace)  asking "is it missing?" is premature -- don't ask yet
A

What if the bank file itself is just late by six hours — real SLA breach, not a bug? Doesn't your window logic raise a false alarm?

B

The window needs a grace buffer beyond the nominal SLA before it escalates to a human alert, and — just as important — a late-arriving file has to be able to re-open a record that already flipped to UNRESOLVED, not get silently dropped because "that case is closed."

Where this leaves us: reconciliation time and event time are different axes. A design that treats "not here right now" as "missing" will generate constant false alarms — correctness here means modeling each source's arrival-time distribution, not just its data format.
Stage 8

Idempotency: the retry that isn't a new event

Architect A

Your webhook endpoint times out once. The PSP retries the same delivery three times. Now you have three ledger events for one payment. What happens to reconciliation?

✎ think first

Where's the earliest point in the pipeline you could tell a retry apart from a genuinely new event — and what breaks if you wait until the matching stage to figure that out?

Reveal the dialoguereveal
B

Without an idempotency key at ingestion, all three retries become three separate ledger rows. When matching runs, one real bank credit now has three candidate ledger rows to pair against — a phantom break, or worse, a phantom double-match.

A

So dedupe during the matching job, right before comparison?

B

Too late — the damage is already sitting in the ledger by then. Dedupe at ingestion, using (source_system, source_event_id) as a hard uniqueness constraint, before the event is ever allowed to reach the recon pipeline.

INGESTION:
  webhook delivery 1: (psp, evt_9F2) --> insert, new
  webhook delivery 2: (psp, evt_9F2) --> reject, duplicate key
  webhook delivery 3: (psp, evt_9F2) --> reject, duplicate key
  Ledger only ever sees ONE event for this payment.
A

What about a genuine double payment — the bank actually credits the account twice by mistake?

B

That's not a delivery duplicate, it's two legitimate source events with two different source_event_ids — both must reconcile independently. The dedupe key is scoped per source's own event id precisely so it never collapses two real, distinct transactions into one.

Where this leaves us: idempotency has to be enforced upstream, at ingestion, per source. Anything that reaches the matching stage as noise stays noise — matching logic can never fully clean up what ingestion let through dirty.
Stage 9

The exception queue

Architect A

You now have UNRESOLVED and BREAK states. Then what — email finance a spreadsheet every morning?

✎ think first

Does every "break" deserve the same response from the same team, or does the right next action depend on why it broke?

Reveal the dialoguereveal
B

A spreadsheet is exactly the "eyeball it at month end" failure mode from stage 0, just automated. It needs a queryable exception queue: every BREAK and UNRESOLVED record classified by break type, and explicitly assigned, not just listed.

A

Why classify break type — isn't "mismatch, please investigate" enough?

B

Because the fix is completely different depending on the type. An amount mismatch on a PolicyBazaar premium needs someone to contact the insurance aggregator; a missing NSDL settlement row needs someone to chase the custodian; a duplicate ledger entry needs an engineer, not finance ops at all.

BREAK TYPE            -->  OWNER / NEXT ACTION
  amount_mismatch       -->  contact counterparty aggregator
  missing_counterpart    -->  chase custodian / settlement team
  status_mismatch        -->  reverify with source system
  duplicate_detected     -->  engineering, check ingestion dedupe
A

Untyped, undifferentiated — what actually happens to that queue in six months?

B

It becomes exactly the spreadsheet nobody reads, just inside a database table instead of Excel — the same failure, with a nicer UI.

Where this leaves us: the exception queue is where stage 4's precision-over-recall bias actually pays off in practice — every unresolved case stays visible and owned, and is never allowed to quietly disappear the way a false match would.
Stage 10

Scale: millions of records a day

Architect A

Mutual fund orders, IPO allotments, insurance premiums — millions a day across all of them. One nightly matching job scans everything. What breaks first?

✎ think first

Matching is fundamentally a per-correlation-key operation. What does that fact suggest about how you should split the work?

Reveal the dialoguereveal
B

Job runtime grows with volume until it can't finish before the next day's data lands — and a single lock over the whole match table serializes work that never needed to be serial in the first place.

A

Most people jump straight to "add more workers." What's the actual unit of independence you're parallelizing across?

B

The correlation key. One key's match decision never depends on another key's data — so partition by source-system + date-window, and match each partition independently, in parallel, with no shared lock between partitions.

BEFORE:                       AFTER:
  1 matching job                 N partitions
  scans everything serially      each keyed by (source, date-window)
                                  matched independently, in parallel
A

A LAMF loan initiated on Day 1 settles on Day 3. Which partition does it belong to — and what happens if you get that wrong?

B

Partition by the correlation key's initiation date, not settlement date, and allow the matching job to look back across a few trailing partitions. A hard calendar-day boundary doesn't match the reality of async settlement — that's the same timing problem from stage 7, showing up again inside the partitioning scheme.

Where this leaves us: scaling recon isn't about faster queries — it's the same coordination-for-parallelism trade-off any high-volume system faces, except here the partition key has to track business semantics, not calendar convenience.
Stage 11

Consistency or availability — for the recon store itself

Architect A

Ingestion writes to the recon store, the matching job reads it, the exception queue reads it — all at once. The store goes briefly unavailable mid-matching-run. What do you do?

✎ think first

What's the actual cost of pausing the matching job for a few minutes, compared to the cost of matching against a partially-written view of the ledger?

Reveal the dialoguereveal
B

Prefer consistency over availability here. Pause the matching job and retry, rather than run it against a store that's mid-write and confidently declare BREAK on data that simply hasn't fully landed.

STORE MID-WRITE:                    RESULT IF MATCHED NOW:
  order O991: half-written row  -->   read sees inconsistent state
                                       matcher flags a false BREAK
                                       -- exactly the outcome stage 4
                                       said we must never produce
A

Doesn't pausing hurt your T+1 SLA?

B

Not meaningfully — matching is already an async, windowed process by design, from stage 7. A few minutes of store unavailability doesn't compound the way it would in a live payment path; this system can afford to wait rather than guess.

Where this leaves us: the correctness-first choice from stage 1 forces the same CAP trade-off here, at the storage layer — this system, unlike the payment rails it's checking, is allowed to be temporarily unavailable. It is never allowed to be confidently wrong.
Stage 12

Can you edit a matched record?

Architect A

A record is MATCHED. Someone finds a data entry error in it later. Do you fix the row?

✎ think first

What does an auditor need to be able to reconstruct six months from now — and does silently editing a matched row make that reconstruction possible or impossible?

Reveal the dialoguereveal
B

No — never mutate a matched record in place. Append a correction event, and derive the current state from the full history, the same way an append-only event log stores history instead of overwriting it.

WRONG (mutate in place):            RIGHT (append correction):
  UPDATE recon SET amount=12500       recon: [MATCHED amount=12000]
  WHERE id=R441;                              [CORRECTION amount=12500]
  -- history of the error is gone      -- both facts survive, in order
A

Isn't a straight UPDATE just simpler? Why does the extra complexity earn its place?

B

Because a matched-then-silently-edited record destroys exactly what audit and compliance rely on: "what did the system believe was true on a given date." That has to be reconstructable precisely, not overwritten by whatever we believe today.

Where this leaves us: this is the same history-vs-state principle that shaped the transaction model back in stage 2, now applied to the recon system's own output — the reconciliation record has to be at least as immutable as the transactions it's judging.
Stage 13

The hardest question: partial and split settlements

Architect A

A customer redeems mutual fund units, and the bank credits it in two separate tranches. One ledger order, two bank credits. Your one-to-one matching just broke. What now?

✎ think first

If matching allows "the amounts happen to sum correctly" as its only rule, what fraud or bug does that quietly open the door to?

Reveal the dialoguereveal
B

Matching can't assume every correlation key maps 1:1. It needs one-to-many and many-to-one support — the sum of N bank credits against one ledger debit, within a tolerance and time window.

LEDGER:  order O991, amount=₹20,000
BANK:    credit C1, amount=₹12,000, ref="RECON:O991"
         credit C2, amount=₹8,000,  ref="RECON:O991"
MATCH:   C1 + C2 == O991, both share the SAME correlation key
A

What stops that from turning into "any combination of transactions whose amounts happen to sum to the target" — which is a fraud and false-positive risk, not a feature?

B

The candidate set has to be constrained before amounts are even considered — only transactions sharing the same correlation key, or an explicit parent-child order reference, are eligible to be aggregated. Amount-sum-matching across otherwise unrelated transactions is never allowed, no matter how neatly the total lines up.

A

Reverse case — one bank settlement batch covering a hundred small ledger orders. Same rule?

B

Same principle, other direction — the batch reference on the bank side must map to a known, declared batch of ledger order ids. It's never inferred purely because the numbers add up.

Where this leaves us: split matching is where the precision-over-recall discipline from stage 4 is tested hardest. The temptation to "just match whatever sums correctly" is precisely the false-positive risk this entire design was built, from stage 0 onward, to prevent.
